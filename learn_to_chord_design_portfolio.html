Objective

This portfolio showcases the design and development of a Music Theory Educational App for iOS devices. Drawing on my formal training at the Baltimore School for the Arts and Peabody Conservatory programs, this project aims to transform music theory learning into an accessible, engaging mobile experience that helps students develop a deeper understanding of harmonic principles.

Through this portfolio, I demonstrate the iterative design process, from initial conceptualization based on learning science principles to user testing and refinement. The project highlights how educational technology can bridge the gap between theoretical knowledge and practical application in music composition and performance.

Project Description & Learning Outcomes

Learn to Chord is designed to help music students strengthen their theoretical understanding through focused learning modules on chord building and resolutions. The mobile platform was chosen deliberately to address the needs of today's music students, who often practice in various locations and benefit from having reference materials readily available.

Learning Outcomes:

const learningOutcomes = [
"Demonstrate understanding of chord construction principles",
"Identify and build various chord types (major, minor, diminished, augmented, seventh) in any key",
"Apply counterpoint theory to create harmonically satisfying chord resolutions",
"Translate theoretical knowledge into practical composition skills"
];

Project Scope:

This iOS application will enable quick practice sessions during otherwise unproductive time periods to facilitate just-in-time learning when working on compositions. The project was prototyped through wireframes created in Figma, followed by a higher fidelity Figma prototype implementing core features including chord visualization and playback functionality.

Learning Science Foundation:

The design incorporates several evidence-based learning science principles:

  • Cognitive Load Theory: Breaking complex music theory concepts into manageable, bite-sized examples that isolate specific chord constructions and resolutions
  • Mayer's Multimedia Learning Principles (segmentation): Presenting complex material in digestible parts to enhance comprehension
  • Modality Principle: Pairing visual chord representations with audio playback to engage multiple learning channels
  • Immediate Feedback: Providing visual and textual cues during practice exercises to reinforce learning

User Testing & Feedback

I conducted user testing sessions to gether initial input for the prototype. Each participant was asked to explore the prototype and complete specific tasks while providing think-aloud feedback.

Testing Methodology:

Each participant was given the following tasks:

  1. Build a C Major chord using the chord builder
  2. Learn about chord resolutions

Key Findings:

Conservatory Graduate Feedback

"The app has a solid foundation but needs more depth for long-term value. I'd quickly outgrow the basic chord functions and want something that challenges me further."

  • Found the interface intuitive but too basic for advanced users
  • Wanted to see theory concepts in the context of actual musical scores
  • Suggested adding more advanced chord types (9ths, 11ths, 13ths)

Music Producer Feedback

"I like the concept, but it feels too classically oriented. I need to see how these concepts apply to jazz harmony and modern chord progressions used in contemporary music."

  • Struggled to find genre-specific applications
  • Wanted more examples from popular music
  • Suggested adding non-classical notation styles (chord charts, Nashville numbering)

Hobby Musician Feedback

"I need to hear what I'm seeing. Without audio playback, it's just abstract shapes to me. Also, why isn't there an explanation for why certain chords work together?"

  • Repeatedly tried to tap chords expecting to hear them
  • Wanted more fundamental explanations of theory concepts
  • Suggested adding a "theory behind this" section to each chord explanation

Common Usability Issues:

  • Audio feedback was the most requested missing feature
  • Genre diversity was important to all users
  • Interface felt clinical and lacked visual appeal
  • Navigation between chord building and progression tools needed improvement
  • Users wanted more explanation of "why" behind theory concepts

Applied Learning Science Principles:

The final design reinforces the application of key learning science principles:

  • Multimodal Learning: Combined visual, auditory, and kinesthetic engagement
  • Just-in-Time Learning: Contextual information available when needed, but not overwhelming
  • Constructivist Approach: Tools to experiment and build understanding through exploration
  • Spaced Repetition: Practice exercises that reinforce concepts over time
